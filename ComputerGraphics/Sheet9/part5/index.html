Sheet9 - Part 5 - Elias

Projection shadows are attractive because they are extremely simple: we analytically project the caster onto a plane, so there is no extra render pass, no additional textures, and the performance cost is tiny. This simplicity also means there are severe limitations. Projection shadows only work for planar receivers, they cannot produce self-shadowing, they always yield hard silhouettes, and the technique breaks down when the scene has multiple receivers or curved surfaces. Shadow mapping, by contrast, renders the scene from the light's perspective and stores depth in a texture. This extra pass and texture memory introduce overhead and bring well-known artifacts such as aliasing, bias tuning, and filtering requirements, yet the benefits outweigh the drawbacks in most general scenes. Shadow mapping supports arbitrary receivers, self-shadowing, and can be extended with filtering to approximate soft shadows, which makes it far more versatile than projection shadows despite its higher implementation and tuning cost.
